<h2 class="text-xl font-semibold">Project Snapshot</h2>

<p>
Between <strong>1970 and 2015</strong>, the world lost roughly
<strong>35% of its coastal wetlands</strong>, driven by urbanisation,
industrialisation, and climate pressures.
</p>

<p>
This project demonstrates how <strong>multi-decadal satellite observation</strong>
combined with geospatial analysis can diagnose ecosystem transformation
in vulnerable coastal landscapes.
</p>

<p>
Two <strong>Ramsar-designated wetlands in Ghana</strong> were analysed:
</p>

<ul class="list-disc pl-6">
<li>Sakumo Catchment</li>
<li>Songor Catchment</li>
</ul>

<p>
Using <strong>30 years of satellite data (1990–2020)</strong>, the study
quantified land-use transitions, ecosystem restructuring, and
socio-environmental drivers of wetland degradation.
</p>

<h2 class="text-xl font-semibold">Intensity Analysis Framework</h2>

<p>
Traditional change detection only reports net gains and losses.
Intensity analysis reveals whether landscape transitions occur
systematically or randomly.
</p>

<ul class="list-disc pl-6 space-y-2">
<li>Identifies disproportionate land transitions</li>
<li>Detects acceleration of landscape change</li>
<li>Distinguishes systematic vs random change</li>
</ul>

</div>

<div>

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/qnspect/IntensityAnalysisFramework.png' | relative_url }}"
class="rounded-xl w-full">

<p class="text-sm text-slate-500 mt-2">
<strong> Intensity analysis framework used to diagnose systematic versus random land-cover transitions.<br></strong>
<i> The three-level structure evaluates landscape change at the interval, category, and transition levels, allowing identification of disproportionately active land conversions. This framework reveals whether wetland losses occur randomly or reflect structured transformations driven by human land-use pressures.
</i>
</p>

<h2 class="text-xl font-semibold">Catchment Boundaries</h2>

<p>
The Sakumo and Songor lagoons are embedded within coastal drainage
basins that define the hydrological limits of surrounding land use
interactions. These catchment boundaries were used to spatially
constrain the analysis of land use and land cover change.
</p>

<p>
A Digital Elevation Model (SRTM) was used to derive terrain structure
and delineate the hydrological catchments draining into each lagoon.
These boundaries provide the spatial framework for analysing land
transformation within the contributing landscape.
</p>

<img src="{{ '/assets/images/qnspect/sksgsubcatchment.png' | relative_url }}"
class="rounded-xl w-full">

<p class="text-sm text-slate-500">
<strong> Hydrologically derived catchment boundaries delineating the contributing drainage areas of the Sakumo and Songor lagoons.<br></strong>
<i> Catchments were extracted from the SRTM DEM using flow direction and accumulation modelling. These boundaries constrain the spatial extent of the land-cover analysis to areas that directly influence lagoon hydrology and wetland ecosystem condition.
</i>
</p>

<div class="space-y-4">

<h3 class="font-semibold">Hydro-Climatic Trends</h3>

<p>
Hydro-climatic variability across the Sakumo and Songor catchments was
evaluated using the <strong>Mann–Kendall trend test</strong> and
<strong>Sen’s slope estimator</strong>, two non-parametric statistical
methods widely used for detecting monotonic trends in environmental
time series. The analysis examined long-term changes in precipitation,
temperature, evapotranspiration, and catchment water balance between
1990 and 2020.
</p>

<p>
Results indicate that <strong>temperature exhibits a statistically
significant upward trend</strong> in both catchments, while rainfall,
evapotranspiration, and overall water balance show no statistically
significant long-term trends.
</p>

<ul class="list-disc pl-6 space-y-2">

<li>
<strong>Temperature:</strong> Significant warming trend detected in both
catchments (p &lt; 0.05) with a Sen’s slope of approximately
<strong>0.03 °C per year</strong>, indicating gradual but persistent
regional warming.
</li>

<li>
<strong>Precipitation:</strong> Mann–Kendall results indicate weak
positive trends; however, the trends are <strong>not statistically
significant</strong> (p &gt; 0.05), suggesting that rainfall variability
remains largely stable over the study period.
</li>

<li>
<strong>Evapotranspiration:</strong> Minor increases were observed but
trend statistics were not significant, indicating no long-term
systematic change in atmospheric water demand.
</li>

<li>
<strong>Catchment Water Balance:</strong> Combined precipitation and
evapotranspiration dynamics show no statistically significant
long-term trend, indicating that large-scale hydro-climatic forcing has
remained relatively stable.
</li>

</ul>

<p>
The statistical results suggest that while regional warming is
detectable, <strong>climate variability alone does not explain the
observed wetland transformation</strong>. Instead, the spatial
correspondence between land-use change and ecosystem degradation
indicates that <strong>anthropogenic land transformation is the
dominant driver of wetland change</strong> within the Sakumo and
Songor catchments.
</p>
