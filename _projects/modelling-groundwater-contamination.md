<h2 class="text-xl font-semibold">Project Context</h2>

<p>
Surface monitoring captures land transformation but rarely resolves
how contaminants propagate below ground. Groundwater systems
transport pollutants through complex geological structures where
hydraulic gradients, permeability contrasts, and pumping stresses
control migration pathways.
</p>

<p>
This project developed a numerical model to investigate how a surface
contaminant source migrates through a layered aquifer system and how
groundwater extraction alters exposure risk.
</p>

<p>
The central question addressed was:
<strong>How does surface disturbance translate into groundwater vulnerability?</strong>
</p>

<h2 class="text-xl font-semibold">Governing Physical Processes</h2>

<p>
Reactive transport within the soil profile is governed by coupled
groundwater flow, solute transport, and chemical transformation
processes. Infiltration generates hydraulic gradients that drive
water movement through the soil matrix, while dissolved contaminants
are redistributed through advection, dispersion, and degradation
reactions.
</p>

<h2 class="text-xl font-semibold">Numerical Model Framework</h2>

<p>
The simulation domain represents a layered aquifer composed of clay,
sand, and gravel units. Contrasting permeability between these layers
controls flow velocity and contaminant migration pathways.
</p>

<ul class="list-disc pl-6 space-y-2">
<li>Clay layers act as low permeability barriers</li>
<li>Sand units provide moderate flow pathways</li>
<li>Gravel zones accelerate groundwater transport</li>
<li>A pumping well generates hydraulic gradients</li>
</ul>

</div>
