Rainfall-Runoff and Non-Point Source Pollution Loads
in the Sakumo Catchment

When it rains in a city, the water doesn’t just vanish—it races off streets and fields, picking up nutrients and pollution along the way. I mapped how 30 years of land use change turned everyday rainfall into a driver of runoff and nutrient loading, showing why wetlands like Sakumo are under pressure and what that means for water, ecosystems, and people.

in Ghana’s Sakumo catchment

Cities don’t just grow upwards—they spill outwards, reshaping land and water. Modelling Groundwater Contamination

Ever wondered how a spill on the ground ends up in a drinking water well? That is exactly what I explored with this COMSOL-based model. I simulated how water and contaminants move underground. Starting from the surface, filtering through soil, and getting pulled into a pumping well. The model uses a combo of physics (Richards’, Darcy, and Stokes) to show just how quickly a well can draw in pollution, even from far away. It is a great visual tool for understanding risk, testing “what-if” scenarios, and planning smarter groundwater protection.

Pesticide Transport and Reaction in Soil

This one dives into the messy afterlife of Aldicarb, a pesticide that does not just disappear once it hits the soil. Using COMSOL, I modeled how it moves, reacts, and breaks down into by-products. Some of which stick around longer and pose more of a threat than the original chemical. The setup mixes soil physics with chemical reactions, showing how things like sorption, leaching, and volatilization play out over time. It is a helpful way to understand the real-world risks of pesticide use and how contaminants can quietly travel beyond where they were first applied.

Terzaghi Compaction

Have you ever wondered what happens underground when you apply weight to saturated soils? A deceptively slow, silent process that has big implications for groundwater systems. I used COMSOL to simulate Terzaghi’s 1D consolidation theory, tracking how excess pore pressure bleeds off over time and causes the soil to compact. Sounds simple, but it mirrors what happens in real-world aquifers under stress—like when overpumping causes land to sink or changes how water flows. This model bridges the gap between geomechanics and hydrogeology, showing that understanding pressure dissipation is not just for engineers. It is critical for anyone trying to manage groundwater sustainably in soft or compressible terrains.
