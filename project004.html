üåç Coastal Wetland Monitoring for Sustainable Development

LULC Change Analysis ‚Ä¢ Remote Sensing

üîç Project Snapshot

From 1970 to 2015 alone, there has been a 35% global loss of coastal wetlands due to unprecedented threats from industrialisation, urbanisation, and climate change. This project demonstrates the application of advanced geospatial technologies to address critical environmental challenges facing coastal wetlands in Ghana. Using two designated Ramsar sites (Sakumo and Songor wetlands) as case studies, this project employed multi-temporal remote sensing data from 1990 to 2020 to quantify land use and land cover (LULC) changes in these ecologically significant areas‚Äîinternational ecosystems recognized for their biodiversity importance.

Map showing the areas of interest (Sakumo & Songor catchments).

üìå Key Objectives

This project aimed to provide an evidence-based synthesis that potentially diagnoses the causes, pace, and mechanics the Sakumo and Songor catchments undergo with the aid of geospatial science. This provides a clearer and comprehensive approach to arrive at informed conservation policies and sustainable land management planning. In achieving this, the following objectives served as milestones;

  • Quantify LULC changes in the Sakumo and Songor catchments from 1990 to 2020,
  • Determine the dynamics of LULC change with intensity analysis, and
  • Evaluate the primary drivers influencing the observed LULC changes by integrating natural and anthropogenic data.

    Data Acquisition & Pre-processing

    For a robust temporal analysis of environmental changes spanning from 1990 to 2020, this research used the Landsat Collection 2 Level 1 satellite imagery (Landsat-4 TM, Landsat-7 ETM, Landsat-8 OLI/TIRS) obtained from the USGS Earth Explorer platform. The 30-year study period allowed for comprehensive tracking of landscape transformations in both wetland catchments. All images underwent rigorous atmospheric correction using the Dark Object Subtraction (DOS) method and were processed to calculate various spectral indices to accurately isolate wetland features. These indices included the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) and Modified Normalized Difference Water Index (MNDWI).

    Additionally, ancillary data such as topographical data (Digital Elevation Model (DEM) from the Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM)) and climatic data (rainfall, temperature, etc.) from remote sources (CHIRPS and TerraClimate) were compared to available ground data from the Ghana Meteorological Agency were integrated to provide context for hydrological modelling. Population data from national censuses and remote datasets were also incorporated to assess anthropogenic pressures.

    Analytical Framework

    The analytical approach incorporated supervised classification techniques with post-classification comparison to quantify changes across different time intervals (~10 years). Four classification algorithms were evaluated: Maximum Likelihood, Random Trees, Support Vector Machine and K-nearest neighbour. The Maximum Likelihood algorithm was selected for its superior accuracy in distinguishing complex wetland habitats.

    The research employed intensity analysis‚Äîa hierarchical framework that reveals not just what changed, but how fast and how systematically those changes occurred. This sophisticated approach allowed for detailed identification of systematic transitions between land categories and provided insights into the dynamics of change processes. The findings were validated through ground-truthing exercises and historical map comparisons, achieving classification accuracy of 72% for Sakumo and 77% for Songor.

    üí° Key Insights

    LULC Change Dynamics

  • The analysis revealed dramatic transformations in both Ramsar sites over the 30-year study period. The Sakumo wetland catchment experienced a significant expansion of built-up areas primarily at the expense of mangrove forests and water bodies. Meanwhile, agricultural land increased, reflecting growing food production demands in the region. The Songor wetland catchment showed slightly different but equally concerning patterns, with 'urban' expansion driven largely by salt mining operations and agricultural development. These anthropogenic and natural factors drove the rate and spate of LULC changes. However, their extents and influence have not been significantly quantified.

    Quantitative analysis of LULC (%) in the Sakumo and Songor catchments (1990-2020). This confirms the trends observed in the change maps and highlight a critical need for conservation frameworks.

  • The intensity analysis demonstrated that changes in the Sakumo wetland catchment were predominantly concentrated in human-induced LULC categories, while the Songor wetland catchment changes were more influenced by natural processes, the anthropogenic factors still played a substantial role. The research attributed these changes primarily to population dynamics and economic pressures, with climate change acting as an additional stressor on these vulnerable ecosystems. The population growth curve correlates strongly with LULC shifts, highlighting the human pressures on the natural resources.
  • To that effect, key drivers influencing the LULC changes in the catchments were assessed. These drivers were population growth and hydro-climatic factors (including temperature, precipitation, evapotranspiration and potential water availability).
  • Local population data (from the Ghana Statistical Service for 2010 and 2021) and remote datasets (from 2000 - 2020 (for Landscan and WorldPop) and from 1990 - 2020 (for GlobPOP)) were compared. Though Sakumo registered a higher population density, Songor showed faster growth rate. This is possibly due to inhabitants migration for greener pastures in urban areas.

    üåø Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services Impacts

    The conversion of natural habitats has led to substantial declines in ecosystem services provided by these wetlands. The loss of mangrove forests has reduced natural buffering capacity against coastal erosion and storm surges, while the degradation of water bodies has diminished their water purification functions and fisheries support capacity. The research documented correlative evidence between LULC changes and declining populations of migratory birds and other wildlife that depend on these habitats, highlighting the conservation implications of these transformations.

    üå¥üè¢ Sustainability Implications: Contributions to Sustainable Development Goals

    This research provides critical insights for achieving multiple Sustainable Development Goals. For SDG 6 (Clean Water and Sanitation), the findings inform strategies for protecting water-related ecosystems. For SDG 13 (Climate Action), the study highlights the role of wetlands in climate change mitigation through carbon sequestration and climate adaptation through buffering extreme weather events. For SDGs 14 and 15 (Life Below Water and Life on Land), the research offers empirical evidence of human impacts on coastal and terrestrial ecosystems, supporting targeted conservation interventions. The catchment-based approach aligns directly with SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities) by demonstrating the connections between urban development patterns and ecosystem health. The research also contributes to SDGs 1 and 2 (No Poverty and Zero Hunger) by documenting how environmental changes affect community livelihoods that depend on wetland resources, highlighting the need for balanced approaches that support both conservation and sustainable use.

    ‚öñÔ∏è Policy Recommendations

    Based on the findings, the study recommended integrated management strategies that address the root causes of wetland degradation while promoting sustainable development. Specific policy interventions include:

  • Zoning regulations that limit urban expansion into ecologically sensitive areas.
  • Incentive programs for sustainable agricultural practices that reduce wetland pollution.
  • Community-based conservation initiatives that engage local stakeholders in wetland management.
  • Climate adaptation plans that incorporate wetland protection as a key strategy.
  • Strengthened governance frameworks that coordinate across different sectors and jurisdictions.
